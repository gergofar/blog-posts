It would be faster to call an API. That's exactly why I don't always do it.

When you wire up a hosted model, you learn its *interface*. When you build one
end to end — data preparation, architecture, training loop, deployment — you
learn its *failure modes*. The second kind of knowledge is the one that keeps
paying off.

## What you only learn by building

- **Where the data really comes from.** Most model problems are data problems
  wearing a model costume.
- **What the loss is actually optimising.** Metrics lie politely; the loss
  tells the truth.
- **Why inference is slow.** You feel every unnecessary copy when you own the
  whole path.

## It keeps leadership honest

As an engineering leader, staying close to the craft isn't nostalgia — it's
how I keep my technical judgment calibrated. It's much harder to set a
realistic roadmap for something you've only ever seen through a dashboard.

So I keep a small habit: every so often, build the thing from scratch in
Python and PyTorch, own the entire lifecycle, and let the rough edges teach
me what the abstractions were hiding.
